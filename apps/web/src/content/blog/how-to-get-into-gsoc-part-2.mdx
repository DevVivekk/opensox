How I prepared for GSoC'24? (and the mistakes I did)

Part-2: Making solid Contributions.

Making solid contributions is the core of GSoC selection. In my opinion, 90% of your selection depends on the quality and quantity of your contributions. But as always, whenever we search on youtube, we get a very repetitive and general answer "make good contributions".

As a beginner, I use to think what the fuck even good contributions means.

After this, I tried using my own mind and came up with this:

I divided the contributions in two aspects. 1. Quality 2. Quantity

## Quality

1. Quality of your contributions matters more than quantity. Fixing two issues that mentor care about is way effective than fixing 1000 translation issues or documentation updates. In the beginning you can pick smaller issues but start picking bigger issues ASAP. (TIP: sometimes I use to pick issues that I had no idea about but I struggled and ultimately fixed. you can try experimenting this as well)

2. First make sure you are delivering the quality code then think about how you can increase the quantity of this. Remember: accuracy >>> speed

3. One problem very often we face is that multiple people try to work on the same issue and it becomes very competitive to get an issue assigned. A best trick that worked for me was to choose complex and difficult issues. I observed most people don't choose difficult and complex issues as they seem hard. So if you choose them you don't have to face the competition. Also when you fix them, mentor will get impressed. (Note: choosing complex issues is really hard so don't do them in the beginning. spend some time in smaller ones and then go aggressive)

4. Don't forget to get the validation from the mentors, if the solution of an issue is very obvious ie. fixing a typo, then you don't need to take validation otherwise for bigger issues, discussion it with mentor, take some validation, only then dive into it. I have seen folks working on an issue for weeks just to know that mentors has no plan to implement that feature. Don't be that guy.

5. I didn't have but if you have good seniors in your college, take guidance from them as well. in-person guidance is way better than any other resource on the internet.

6. Join the organisation early. If you join the organisation early you'll have more time to solve lots of issues. Also understanding the codebase takes time (specially for beginners). So if you join early (sept-oct), you'll be able to go aggressive and fix complex issues by the peak time (jan-feb) while others still introducing themselves in the community.

7. Lastly, just persist. Sometimes while fixing the issues, you'll feel like your mind is bleeding and about to blow up. It happens. keep on doing it. Things become easy by the time.

## Quantity

As I said before, keep the quality in check and then pull as much quantity as you can without disturbing the quality.
